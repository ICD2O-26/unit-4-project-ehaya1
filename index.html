Artificial General Intelligence

When machines stop being tools — and start becoming minds.

What Is AGI?

Most of the AI we use today — image recognition, spam filters, large language models — is called Narrow AI: software that is superhuman at one specific task but helpless at anything else. Ask a chess engine to write a poem and it fails. Ask a chatbot to drive a car and it has no idea where to begin.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) is different in kind, not just degree. An AGI system would be able to learn any intellectual task a human can — planning, creativity, emotional reasoning, scientific discovery — without being explicitly programmed for each one. It would transfer knowledge between domains, set its own goals, and improve through experience, just as people do.

"The development of full artificial intelligence could spell the end of the human race… or it could be the best thing ever to happen to us. It's important to get the development right." — Stephen Hawking, physicist (paraphrased from 2014 BBC interview)
Quick Fact In a 2022 survey of leading AI researchers, the median estimate for when there is a 50% chance of AGI arriving was 2059 — though estimates ranged from 2030 to "never." The debate is still very much open.

AGI vs. Narrow AI — A Simple Comparison

The table below shows the key differences at a glance.

Feature Narrow AI (today) AGI (future goal)
Scope One task only Any intellectual task
Learning From labelled training data From experience & reasoning
Transfer Cannot transfer skills Transfers knowledge freely
Examples ChatGPT, AlphaGo, Siri Theoretical; does not exist yet
Human-level? Only in its narrow domain Across all domains

How Does AGI Roughly Work?

AGI does not exist yet, but researchers are actively building the building blocks. Three major approaches are being pursued:

  1. Scaling Large Language Models (LLMs) — Some researchers believe that making today's neural networks much larger, and training them on much more data, will eventually produce general reasoning. Models like GPT-4 and Claude already show surprising emergent abilities.
  2. Neurosymbolic AI — Combining the pattern-recognition of neural networks with the logical rule-following of symbolic systems. The goal is a system that can both feel what is probably true and prove why it is true.
  3. Whole-Brain Emulation — Mapping and simulating the human brain at a neuronal level, then running it in software. This approach is extremely far away given current hardware limits.
▸ What is a neural network? (click to expand)

A neural network is a mathematical system loosely inspired by the brain. It consists of layers of simple units called neurons. Each neuron receives numbers as input, multiplies them by learned weights, and passes the result forward. During training, the weights adjust automatically so that the network's output matches the correct answer. Stack millions of neurons across hundreds of layers, train on billions of examples, and you get a modern LLM like Claude or GPT-4.

▸ What would make an AI "general"? (click to expand)

Most researchers agree an AGI would need to demonstrate:

  • Transfer learning — using knowledge from one field in another
  • Causal reasoning — understanding why things happen, not just that they happen
  • Common sense — knowing that ice is cold without being told
  • Meta-cognition — knowing what it does and doesn't know
  • Goal-directed behaviour — setting and pursuing multi-step plans autonomously

Where Is AGI Research Today?

AGI itself does not exist, but the race to build it is very real. Several industries and organisations are investing heavily:

🔬 Technology & Research Labs

OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and Meta AI are the main private labs. OpenAI has stated that building AGI safely is its explicit mission. Google DeepMind's CEO Demis Hassabis has said AGI could arrive "within a few years." These labs employ thousands of researchers and spend billions of dollars annually on compute alone.

🏥 Healthcare

Pre-AGI AI is already diagnosing cancers, predicting protein structures (AlphaFold), and discovering new drug compounds. An AGI could run autonomous clinical trials, personalise treatment in real time, and accelerate drug discovery from decades to months.

⚖️ Government & Policy

Canada, the EU, the US, and the UK have all launched national AI strategies. The UK AI Safety Institute and the US NIST AI framework are attempting to set safety standards before AGI arrives — a challenging task when the technology is still theoretical.

🎓 Education

Today's AI tutors (like Khan Academy's Khanmigo) are Narrow AI. An AGI tutor could adapt to a student's exact learning style, emotional state, and knowledge gaps in real time — potentially replacing one-size-fits-all curricula entirely.

Social Impacts of AGI

The impacts of AGI are unlike any previous technology because AGI would not just do tasks — it would think. That changes everything.

Economic Impacts

A 2023 Goldman Sachs report estimated that AI could automate 300 million full-time jobs globally within a decade. AGI would accelerate this far beyond Narrow AI. Jobs requiring creativity, judgment, and emotional intelligence — once considered safe — would not be immune. However, history shows that automation also creates new kinds of jobs. The net effect on employment is the subject of serious, ongoing debate among economists.

Ethical & Safety Impacts

The most discussed risk is called misalignment: an AGI that pursues goals that are subtly different from what humans intended. AI safety researcher Stuart Russell uses this analogy: "You can't fetch coffee if you're dead — but you can't fetch coffee if the humans who gave you the goal are dead either." Getting the goal specification exactly right is an unsolved problem.

  • Bias at scale — If an AGI is trained on biased human data, it could enforce inequalities at a speed and scale no human institution could match.
  • Concentration of power — Whoever controls AGI first gains an enormous geopolitical and economic advantage, raising fears of a new kind of monopoly.
  • Autonomous weapons — Militaries around the world are exploring AI-controlled weapons. An AGI applied to warfare creates risks of conflict at machine speed, far faster than human decision-making.
  • Privacy & surveillance — An AGI could process and correlate all publicly available data about a person, ending practical anonymity.

Cultural & Social Impacts

Humans have always defined themselves partly by cognitive uniqueness — the ability to create art, solve problems, empathise. An AGI that matches or exceeds all of these abilities forces a profound question: what makes us uniquely human? Psychologists warn of identity crises at a societal level. Philosophers are already debating whether an AGI could deserve rights.

Environmental Impacts

Training large AI models today requires enormous amounts of electricity. GPT-4's training is estimated to have used as much energy as hundreds of US homes consume in a year. An AGI — far more complex — could have a significant carbon footprint unless powered by renewable energy. Water usage for data-centre cooling is also a growing concern.

▸ Local Impact: What Does This Mean for Canada?

Canada is a global leader in AI research — Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, and Yann LeCun (the so-called "Godfathers of Deep Learning") all have deep ties to Canadian universities. The Canadian Institute for Advanced Research (CIFAR) funds major AI safety work.

Locally, AGI could reshape sectors that are central to the Canadian economy: natural resources (autonomous extraction), healthcare (a sector with chronic staffing shortages), and finance. Canada's Pan-Canadian AI Strategy is intended to keep Canada competitive while developing the technology responsibly.

▸ Positive Possibilities — What Could AGI Solve?

It is easy to focus on risk. The potential benefits are also extraordinary:

  • Solving climate change by designing better energy systems and materials
  • Curing diseases that have resisted human medicine for centuries
  • Providing world-class education to every child on Earth, for free
  • Accelerating scientific discovery across every field simultaneously

Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei has written that AGI could compress "50–100 years of biological progress into 5–10 years." The stakes — in both directions — could not be higher.

My Reflection

Researching AGI made me realise that the most important questions around this technology are not technical — they are human. We already have the mathematics to build increasingly powerful AI. What we do not yet have is agreement on what values that AI should hold, who gets to decide, and how we make sure the benefits are shared fairly.

I found the alignment problem especially striking. It is not enough to build a system that is intelligent — it must be intelligent in the right direction. And defining "right" for a machine that could be smarter than any human is one of the hardest problems anyone has ever tried to solve.

The question is not whether we can build AGI. The question is whether we are wise enough to decide if we should — and careful enough to do it safely if we do. — Personal reflection, Eliza Hayat
