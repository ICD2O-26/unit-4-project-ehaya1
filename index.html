Artificial General Intelligence

A theoretical analysis of autonomous cognitive systems and cross-domain reasoning.

Conceptual Definition

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) represents a pivot from narrow, task-specific algorithms to Universal Competence. Unlike current models, AGI possesses the ability to transfer knowledge across disparate fields—applying mathematical logic to creative arts or scientific discovery without intervention.

// KEY METRICS

Timeline: 2029 — 2045
Core Mechanism: Generalization
Current State: Proto-AGI
Risk Factor: High Alignment Required

Logical Framework

The development of AGI is predicated on the Scaling Hypothesis, which suggests that intelligence is an emergent property of sufficient computational power and data diversity.

Technical Appendix: Neural Plasticity

Artificial neural networks mimic biological structures, allowing for "weights" between connections to be adjusted through backpropagation, creating a system that learns from experience rather than code.

Societal Implications

The transition to AGI involves a fundamental restructuring of global labor. As cognitive tasks become automated, the value of Human-Centric Ethics becomes the primary concern for world leaders.

Economic Shifts

The total automation of intellectual labor leads to the necessity of new fiscal models, such as Universal Basic Income, to sustain post-labor societies.

The Alignment Problem

The ethical risk of AGI involves ensuring that the machine's terminal goals are mathematically verified to align with human survival and flourishing.
